In this paper the search for a significant improvement of both of these parameters, and therefore of the doubling time, is aimed to a design of a molten chloride fast breeder reactor, with as good as possible doubling time characteristics.
This can be achieved by rather trivial improvements. But here it must be stressed that most of these improvements can be realised only in a fast reactor with liquid fuel and especially in molten plutonium chlorides.

This preliminary report gives some selected datas about such a type of power reactor.

In a typical liquid metal fast breeder and in gas cooled fast breeder this value equals: $120\mathrm{KeV}$ and $176\mathrm{KeV}$ respectively.

Impact of the plutonium contents in the fuel

One of the most important problems in achieving a high breeding ratio seems to be the hardness of the neutron flux, this is strongly influenced by the composition of the fuel.

In this case the fuel has been postulated to be a mixture of

a $\mathrm{PuCl}_3\cdot \mathrm{bNaCl}\cdot \mathrm{cUCl}_3$

$$
a = 0, 1 - 0, 2 \quad b = 0, 7 - 0, 8 \quad c = 0, 1 - 0, 2
$$

Unfortunately not all datas for this system are available (fig. 5).

The rough calculation of the changing concentration of $\mathrm{PuCl}_3$ in the melt with NaCl (fig. 6) shows a rather sharp decrease of breeding gain BG for decreasing plutonium concentration, especially when the plutonium molar ratio to the sodium is lower than 0.25.

In spite of these uncertainties of the $\mathrm{PuCl}_3$ -NaCl-UCl₃ system here has been calculated the impact of uranium-238 in the fuel. For a constant $\mathrm{PuCl}_3$ concentration, with simplified correction of NaCl concentration, the results are given on the fig. 7.

For the increasing ratio of uranium to plutonium in fuel from 0 to 3 the total breeding gain increases from 0,65 to 0,95. It is a rather clear situation; and therefore the reference reactor includes uranium in fuel in a ratio of 2:1 to the plutonium.

# Impact of F.P. concentration

This parameter plays a very important role. For given reactor design and given fuel and fertile composition the increase the concentration of F.P. (here simulated by Cs-133 only) from $2 \cdot 10^{-5}$ to $2 \cdot 10^{-4}$ (in $10^{24} / \mathrm{cm}^3$ ) decreases the breeding gain from 0,65 up to 0,38, when specific power decreases less than twice.

In steady state reactor a concentration of $2 \cdot 10^{-5}$ atoms F.P. $10^{24} / \mathrm{cm}^3$ for a fuel with $2,1 \cdot 10^{-3}$ atoms Pu $\cdot 10^{24} / \mathrm{cm}^3$ is to achieve for a specific power of $2 \mathrm{KW} / \mathrm{cm}^3$ after a time period of

$$
t = \frac {2 \cdot 1 0 ^ {- 5} \cdot 1 0 ^ {2 4}}{2 \cdot 1 0 ^ {3} \cdot 3 , 1 \cdot 1 0 ^ {1 0} \cdot 2} = 1, 6 1 \cdot 1 0 ^ {5} \sec
$$

that is after 1,87 days. The higher value of F.P. concentration that is $2 \cdot 10^{-4}$ corresponds to 18,7 days of mean dwelling time of fuel in reactor.

Impact of chlor-37 separation a separated chlorine Cl-37 which has much lower absorption cross section than Cl-35. The impact of each adsorber on the breeding ratio is given by:

$$
\Delta B = \frac {A + D + L + \alpha}{1 + \alpha}
$$

$\Delta \mathrm{B} =$ decrement of breeding ratio

A = absorption rate in given absorber

D absorption rate in rest of absorbers

L = leakage

$\alpha = \sigma c / \sigma f$

Because in typical case for strong absorber in hard spectrum fast core

$$
A \cong (D + L) \cong \alpha = 0. 1 5
$$

The relativ impact on the rather high breeding ratio of $B = 1.6$ results in a case when the "profit" of the separation factor will be e.g. 0,9 A, than

$$
\Delta B = \frac {0 , 9 \cdot 0 , 1 5}{1 , 1 5} = 0, 1 2
$$

and in relation to breeding gain

$$
\Delta G = \frac {0 , 1 2}{0 , 6} = 0, 2 0
$$

doubling time $\frac{T^{\prime}2}{T_{2}} = \frac{G}{G + \Delta G} = 0.83$
